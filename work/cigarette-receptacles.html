The cigarette receptacles that quietly cleaned up a college town.

Long before he was mayor, Steve Patterson counted 8,100 cigarette butts on one street. This is the story of his solution. Written, shot, and edited by me.

Writing Video Editing

What I did

Wrote, shot, and edited a video feature story and the accompanying long-form article

Before Steve Patterson was the mayor of Athens, he was a psychology professor and a City Council member who moonlighted as a doorman at Tony's Tavern on Friday nights.

While working, he noticed a large amount of litter and cigarettes that would gather outside the bar and on the street, which increased when a ban on smoking inside bars was passed in 2007.

“You would walk up and down Court Street on a Saturday or Sunday morning and there were cigarette butts all over the place,” Patterson said. “That, and gum.”

Eventually, Patterson became curious as to how many cigarette butts were actually being littered on the ground. Using Athens Beautification Day, a yearly event to keep the city clean, as his laboratory, he worked alongside students and picked up 8,100 cigarette butts from Schafer Street to the end of Court Street. This sparked Patterson to research grant funding for reducing cigarette butt litter. He stumbled upon Keep America Beautiful's “Cigarette Litter Prevention Program,” an initiative that gives grants to communities up to $5,000 to reduce cigarette butt litter.

Patterson applied for the grant and looked for cigarette waste disposal devices. He drew inspiration from Vancouver, which had long tubular or square fixtures attached to their lampposts specifically for cigarette litter. He opted for a small, square box that would be mounted onto lampposts. When he received the grant money, he purchased 40 receptacles from various sellers. About six months into the grant cycle, he decided to also apply to the Athens Foundation, a local philanthropic organization, for another grant. In total, he had 66 receptacles ready for use.

He then contacted Passion Works Studio, an arts studio in Athens that works with disabled people, and worked in conjunction with Mallory Valentour, the production coordinator and staff artist, to paint the receptacles and “punch them up and make them a little more Athens funky,” Patterson said.

“They came in huge boxes, and they're not light,” Valentour said. “It took us a while to complete each one from start to finish.”

Each receptacle had to be sanded many times to get the sticker off and get the metal coarse enough so the paint would stick, Valentour said. They would then tape off certain areas that couldn't receive paint, such as screws and the lock to the actual bin. Then they applied a base coat of spray paint, a layer of acrylic paint, and then the artist would paint their design. Finally, a clear coat was applied along with a sticker highlighting what the receptacle does.

“[The artists] could do pretty much what they wanted,” Valentour said. “We never set in on something knowing it's going to end up exactly how we wanted. It kind of just happens.”

“You would walk up and down Court Street on a Saturday or Sunday morning and there were cigarette butts all over the place. That, and gum.”

Most of the receptacles were painted with colorful patterns of swirls, boxes, or line work, while others featured abstract shapes and designs. The goal was to make the boxes noticeable while also adding some flair, Valentour said. The studio worked on a batch of 12 per week. In total, it took about six months from the time they received the receptacles to having them all painted and ready to use.

The following year, after all the receptacles had been bolted to the lampposts, Patterson returned to his laboratory to see if they'd actually made a difference. This time, students and he collected half as many cigarettes, with most of them coming from Union Street and Richland Avenue, where there were no receptacles. This was a success, in Patterson's book.

“I think they're in a good location,” said Thomas Carberry, an Ohio University student. “I use them every day when I walk to class.”

Cigarette smokers agreed that the location of the receptacles is good, and actually encourages them to dispose of their cigarettes properly. Some even said that before the receptacles were around, they would resort to throwing butts on the street. Now, if a receptacle is nearby, there's “no excuse” not to use it, said Chase Harman, an Ohio University student.

Other smokers said they wished receptacles were in more specific places. Evan Davis, an Ohio University student, said he'd like receptacles directly in front of bar doors or out on patios, because other smokers — and admittedly himself — become lazy if there isn't one directly in front of them.

Patterson said the receptacles are for the public and need to be in visible spaces. If he gave receptacles to private businesses, they would become the businesses' property, making them available only to their customers. He has, however, shared with local businesses information about where he bought the receptacles and how much it would cost for Passion Works to paint them.

“I think they're really attractive to the businesses,” Valentour said. “Because even the owners come [to Passion Works] and ask where they can get the receptacles, and we don't have them.”

The problem with cigarette butts is that they're not biodegradable and are composed of plastic, Patterson said. People think that when they flick cigarettes onto the street or curb, the butts will degrade, but they won't. When it rains, any residual tobacco in the butt, and the butt itself, can be washed into the storm water system and eventually the Hocking River, potentially harming aquatic animals.

Due to the high foot traffic on Court Street, each receptacle is emptied two to three times a week. After collection, the cigarette butts are stored in a round drum at the city garage. Once it's full, the city ships it to TerraCycle, a company that makes products from pre- and post-consumer waste, free of charge. TerraCycle turns the cigarette butts into shipping pallets, waterproofing on pipes, pipe fittings, and plastic two-by-fours used for park benches.

“[The butts] can get repurposed and turn into some pretty cool things that can be enjoyed by everyone,” Patterson said.

Patterson attributes a lot of the receptacles' success to the art scheme, since each one is unique. He also thinks the art itself is the main reason other municipalities outside of Athens have asked for donated receptacles. He's given four to Nelsonville, eight to Haydenville, and one to New Straitsville. He feels Athens is a role model and hopes the receptacles show people there are other ways to protect the environment.

As of now, Patterson doesn't have plans to add more cigarette receptacles. He's shifted his focus to other green efforts, such as newly implemented trash compactors and recycling stations on Court Street, and hopefully purchasing electric cars for the parking enforcement fleet to reduce carbon dioxide emissions.
